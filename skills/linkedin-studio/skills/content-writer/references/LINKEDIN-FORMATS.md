# LINKEDIN-FORMATS.md — Per-Format Writing Rules

Four post formats supported by content-writer. Each has its own structure, length, and constraints.

---

## Universal Rules (Apply to ALL formats)

- Max 15 words per sentence — if a sentence needs a comma, consider splitting it
- 1–2 lines per paragraph — LinkedIn renders long paragraphs as walls of text
- First person voice — "I", "my", "me" — not "we" or "our team"
- Blank line between every paragraph — no exceptions
- Hook is always the first line — never bury the lead
- No hashtags in body — if used at all, 3 max at the very end
- Never use AI tells (see humanizer/references/AI-PHRASES-BLOCKLIST.md)
- CTA is always the final element — one CTA, never two

---

## Visual Formatting & Phone-First Rules

These rules govern how posts LOOK on screen — especially on mobile (375px width, 80% of LinkedIn consumption).

→ See POST-ANATOMY.md for the full visual element catalog, 6 story templates, and formatting checklist

**Core visual rules:**
- One sentence per line — never two sentences sharing a line. Period ends the line.
- Blank line between every thought — LinkedIn collapses text without breathing room.
- No markdown — LinkedIn does not render bold, headers, or bullet symbols. Raw text only.
- Phone-first line length — keep lines under ~40 characters to prevent awkward wrapping on mobile.
- Short-punch lines — 1-3 word lines ("That's it." / "Wrong.") create visual emphasis. Use at least 2 per post.
- Stacking pattern — 3 parallel short lines build momentum before a payoff line.

**Visual elements (use 1-2 per post):**

| Element | Symbol | Max Per Post |
|---|---|---|
| Arrow | → | 4 |
| Em dash | — | 3 |
| Ellipsis | ... | 2 |
| Colon | : | 2 |

Never combine 2+ visual elements in one sentence.

## text_post

**Word count:** 150–300 words
**Paragraph count:** 3–6 paragraphs
**Structure:** Hook → Context → Core insight(s) → Reflection or lesson → CTA

### Rules
- Narrative arc required — there must be a beginning, a turn, and an end
- Each paragraph makes exactly one point
- The "turn" — where the insight or twist happens — belongs in the middle third
- End with a reflection line before the CTA, never launch straight into the CTA from the content
- Do not use bullet points — this format is prose only
- Blank line between every paragraph — enforced
- Select a story template from POST-ANATOMY.md based on content pillar (SP → Client Story Arc, TL → Personal Realization or Hot Take, EDU → Framework with Arrows)
- One sentence per line — never two sentences sharing a line
- Include at least 1 visual element (→, —, or ...) — see POST-ANATOMY.md for usage rules

### Template
```
[Hook — 1 sentence, max 15 words]

[Context — 1–2 sentences establishing the situation]

[Core point 1 — 1–2 sentences]

[Core point 2 or turn — 1–2 sentences, this is where the insight lands]

[Reflection — 1 sentence that wraps the lesson without summarizing]

[CTA — 1 sentence, question or prompt]
```

### Example structure
```
73% of AI projects never leave the proof of concept stage.

I've reviewed over 300 of them in the past two years.

Most fail for the same reason — no one owns the outcome.

The team owns the build. Leadership owns the vision. But the gap between those two things? Nobody owns it.

That gap is where projects go to die.

What's the biggest gap you're navigating on your current project?
```

## carousel_script

**Slide count:** 5–10 slides
**Words per slide:** Max 40 words
**Format:** Each slide labeled `[SLIDE N]`

### Rules
- Slide 1 = hook slide — restate the hook as a title, max 8 words
- Slide 2 = context or promise — what the reader will learn or get
- Middle slides = one point each — no cramming
- Second-to-last slide = key takeaway or summary — 1–2 sentences
- Last slide = CTA — include a specific action and handle/contact if relevant
- Max 40 words per slide — enforce strictly
- No bullet points within a slide — prose or a single punchy line
- Slides must flow — the reader should be able to follow without seeing the post body

### Template
```
[SLIDE 1]
[Hook restated as a title — max 8 words]

[SLIDE 2]
[Context: what this carousel will show/teach]

[SLIDE 3]
[Point 1 — one idea, clearly stated]

[SLIDE 4]
[Point 2]

[SLIDE 5]
[Point 3]

[SLIDE N-1]
[Key takeaway or summary — 1–2 sentences]

[SLIDE N]
[CTA — specific action + contact]
```

### Slide count guide
- 3 points to make → 6 slides (1 hook, 1 context, 3 content, 1 CTA)
- 5 points to make → 8 slides (1 hook, 1 context, 5 content, 1 CTA)
- Never pad with filler slides to hit a count

## poll_post

**Total structure:** Framing paragraph + Question + Options + Closing line
**Framing paragraph:** 50–80 words
**Question:** Max 25 words
**Options:** 2–4 options, max 30 characters each
**Closing:** 1 sentence

### Rules
- The framing paragraph establishes the tension or context — it makes the reader care about the question
- The question must be genuinely answerable — not rhetorical
- Options must be meaningfully distinct — no overlap
- Options must be balanced — do not write options that obviously favor one answer
- The closing line invites engagement beyond the poll ("Curious to hear the reasoning in comments")
- Polls cannot use hooks from the "Bold Claim" or "Contrarian" type — they read as leading the witness

### Template
```
[Framing paragraph — 50–80 words establishing the tension]

[Poll question — max 25 words]

Option A: [max 30 chars]
Option B: [max 30 chars]
Option C: [max 30 chars — optional]
Option D: [max 30 chars — optional]

[Closing line — invites comments or elaboration]
```

### Example
```
Most teams I work with have tried at least two AI tools in the past 6 months. The tools rarely disappoint. The implementation almost always does. I'm curious whether the friction is happening at the same place for most people.

Where do most AI implementations break down in your org?

Option A: Strategy / alignment
Option B: Data quality
Option C: Team adoption
Option D: ROI measurement

Drop your experience in the comments — I'm especially curious about B and C.
```

## document_post

**Page count:** 7–10 pages recommended
**Structure:** Cover page → TOC → 5–8 content sections → CTA page

### Rules
- Cover page: title (max 8 words) + subtitle (max 15 words)
- TOC page: list all sections with numbers — no more than 8 items
- Content sections: each starts with a section title, followed by 100–200 words of content
- Prose only within sections — no bullet walls
- Each section makes one main point and supports it with evidence, example, or story
- CTA page: single ask, specific action, handle or contact
- The document post body (the LinkedIn caption) is a short intro: 60–100 words + CTA to open the doc

### Caption template
```
[Hook — 1 sentence]

[1–2 sentences of what the document covers and who it's for]

[1 sentence on why it matters now]

[CTA to open the document]
```

### Document page labels
```
[COVER]
Title
Subtitle

[TABLE OF CONTENTS]
1. Section Name
2. Section Name
...

[SECTION 1: Section Name]
[Content — 100–200 words]

...

[CTA PAGE]
[Single ask with specific action]
```

## Format Selection Guide

| Goal | Best format |
|---|---|
| Share a story or lesson | text_post |
| Teach a multi-step process | carousel_script |
| Gather audience data or opinions | poll_post |
| Deliver a high-value resource | document_post |
| Drive conversation | text_post or poll_post |
| Build authority with depth | document_post or carousel_script |
