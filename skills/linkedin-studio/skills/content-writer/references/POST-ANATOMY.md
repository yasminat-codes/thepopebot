# POST-ANATOMY.md — Visual Formatting, Story Templates, and Phone-First Rules

This is the visual formatting reference for content-writer. Every post generated by this skill must pass through these rules before output. LinkedIn is a mobile-first platform — formatting is not decoration, it is readability. A poorly formatted post with great content will still get scrolled past.

Use this file to validate structure, select visual elements, and apply story-driven templates to every post.

---

## 1. Visual Formatting Rules

These rules are non-negotiable. Every post must follow all six.

### One sentence per line.

Never put two sentences on the same line. When a sentence ends with a period, question mark, or exclamation point, the next sentence starts on the next line. This forces the reader's eye to move down the screen rather than across, which increases reading speed and reduces cognitive load on mobile.

### Blank line between every thought.

LinkedIn's renderer collapses consecutive lines into a single paragraph if there is no blank line between them. Two lines that look separate in your editor will smash together in the feed. Always insert a blank line between every logical thought, every sentence pair, every section shift.

### No markdown.

LinkedIn does not render bold (`**`), headers (`##`), bullets (`-`), or any other markdown syntax. They show up as raw characters in the feed and look broken. Use raw text only. For emphasis, use short lines, visual elements (arrows, em dashes), and line breaks.

### Phone-first.

The dominant screen width is 375px. Lines over approximately 40 characters will wrap mid-word or mid-phrase on mobile, creating awkward visual breaks the writer did not intend. Keep lines short. When in doubt, read the post on a phone simulator before finalizing.

### Short paragraphs punch.

Lines of 1-3 words create visual emphasis that longer sentences cannot achieve. They act as punctuation for the entire post. Use them after a setup to land a point.

Examples of punch lines:
- "That's it."
- "Wrong."
- "Every single time."
- "Nobody told me."
- "Read that again."

### Stacking pattern.

Three parallel short lines build momentum before a payoff line. The rhythm creates a drumbeat effect — the reader feels the acceleration and expects a landing.

Example:
```
Not the tools.
Not the tech stack.
Not the budget.

The bottleneck was a 14-step approval process nobody questioned.
```

## 2. Visual Element Catalog

| Element | Symbol | Use For | Max Per Post | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Arrow | → | Transformation, flow, progression | 4 | "Manual process → automated" |
| Em dash | — | Emphasis, aside, abrupt clarification | 3 | "One metric — that's it." |
| Ellipsis | ... | Trailing thought, suspense | 2 | "And they wonder why it fails..." |
| Colon | : | Setup before a reveal | 2 | "The result: 6 hours became 20 minutes." |
| Line break in sentence | (newline) | Emphasis on the final words | 2 | "She looked at the dashboard and said\nthis changes everything." |
| Parenthetical aside | (text) | Conversational texture, dry humor | 1 | "(Nobody talks about this part.)" |

### Visual Element Rules

- **Never combine 2+ visual elements in one sentence.** No "→ ... —" in a single line. Each element carries its own weight. Stacking them dilutes all of them.

- **Arrows work best in before/after and list contexts.** They imply transformation and forward motion. Use them when showing a state change or walking through sequential steps.

- **Em dashes work best for interrupting yourself with a stronger point.** The reader expects the sentence to continue one way, and the em dash redirects. That surprise is the emphasis.

- **Ellipses create suspense — use at the end of a setup line, not mid-sentence.** An ellipsis mid-sentence reads as uncertainty. An ellipsis at the end of a line reads as "wait for it." Place them at line endings only.

## 3. Story-Driven Post Templates

Six templates covering all four content pillars. Each template includes structure, a complete example post, and a structural breakdown.

---

### Template 1: Client Story Arc

**Pillar:** SP (Social Proof)

**Structure:** Hook → Client problem → What we found → What we built → The result → Lesson → CTA

**Flow:**
```
Hook (1-2 lines)
↓
Client problem (3-4 lines)
↓
What we found (2-3 lines)
↓
What we built (3-4 lines)
↓
The result (2-3 lines with specific numbers)
↓
Lesson (1-2 lines)
↓
CTA (1-2 lines)
```

**Complete Example Post:**

```
A med spa owner told me she was working 70 hours a week.

Not because business was slow.

Because business was booming and her systems weren't.

Every new client meant a phone call.
Every phone call meant a voicemail.
Every voicemail meant a callback that happened 6 hours too late.

She was losing 30% of her leads before she ever spoke to them.

When I looked under the hood, I found 4 people doing work that one workflow could handle.

Appointment requests came in through a web form.
Someone copied them into a spreadsheet.
Someone else texted the client.
A third person updated the calendar.

We built an AI intake system in 11 days.

Form submission → instant text confirmation → calendar booking → automated follow-up sequence.

No spreadsheet.
No voicemail.
No 6-hour gap.

Her lead conversion went from 22% to 61% in the first month.

She called me on a Tuesday afternoon and said she left work at 3 PM for the first time in two years.

She almost didn't believe it.

The lesson: most small businesses don't need more marketing.

They need to stop losing the people already walking through the door.

If your team is drowning in admin work they didn't sign up for, I'd love to hear what's eating your hours.
```

**Structural Breakdown:**

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | "A med spa owner told me..." | Specific, human, earns the click |
| Client problem | "Every new client meant..." through "...spoke to them" | Stacking pattern builds the pain |
| What we found | "When I looked under the hood..." through "...updated the calendar" | Concrete details prove expertise |
| What we built | "We built an AI intake system..." through "No 6-hour gap" | Solution as transformation, arrows show flow |
| The result | "Her lead conversion..." through "...didn't believe it" | Specific numbers + human moment |
| Lesson | "The lesson: most small businesses..." | Reusable insight, not self-promotion |
| CTA | "If your team is drowning..." | Invitational, not salesy |

### Template 2: Personal Realization

**Pillar:** TL (Thought Leadership)

**Structure:** Old belief → The event that changed it → New understanding → How it applies → CTA

**Pattern:** "I used to think X. Then Y happened. Now I know Z."

**Flow:**
```
Old belief (2-3 lines)
↓
The event (4-6 lines with specific detail)
↓
New understanding (2-3 lines)
↓
How it applies broadly (3-4 lines)
↓
CTA (1-2 lines)
```

**Complete Example Post:**

```
I used to think the best AI automations were the most complex ones.

More steps.
More logic branches.
More edge cases handled.

Then a bakery owner showed me her "system."

She had a notebook.

Every morning she opened it, looked at yesterday's sales, and decided what to bake.

40 years of pattern recognition in a spiral-bound notebook.

I built her a simple daily report — yesterday's sales, weather forecast, and local events — delivered to her phone at 5 AM.

No chatbot.
No dashboard.
No 47-step workflow.

Just the information she already used, arriving before she needed it.

Her waste dropped 18% in one month.

That notebook taught me something I now tell every client.

The goal is never to replace how someone thinks.

The goal is to feed their thinking faster.

The best automation doesn't feel like technology.

It feels like someone who knows you did you a favor before you asked.

What's the simplest system you've seen outperform a complex one?
```

**Structural Breakdown:**

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Old belief | "I used to think..." through stacking pattern | Sets up the reversal |
| The event | "Then a bakery owner..." through "...spiral-bound notebook" | Specific, surprising, human |
| New understanding | "I built her a simple daily report..." through "...before she needed it" | The pivot moment |
| How it applies | "That notebook taught me..." through "...before you asked" | Universal principle from specific story |
| CTA | "What's the simplest system..." | Invites engagement, not selling |

### Template 3: Before/After Transformation

**Pillar:** SP/TL (Social Proof + Thought Leadership)

**Structure:** Before state → transition → After state → How → CTA

Uses → arrows as visual transformation markers.

**Flow:**
```
Setup (1-2 lines)
↓
Before state (3-5 lines using → arrows)
↓
Transition line (1 line)
↓
After state (3-5 lines using → arrows)
↓
How we did it (2-3 lines)
↓
CTA (1-2 lines)
```

**Complete Example Post:**

```
6 weeks changed everything for a property management company with 200 units.

Before:

Tenant request → email to office manager → office manager assigns to maintenance → maintenance calls tenant → tenant misses call → voicemail loop begins

Average resolution: 4.2 days.

Tenant satisfaction: 2.1 out of 5.

After:

Tenant request → AI triage in 30 seconds → auto-dispatch to right vendor → tenant gets real-time status updates → issue closed

Average resolution: 14 hours.

Tenant satisfaction: 4.4 out of 5.

Same team.
Same budget.
Same buildings.

The only thing that changed was how information moved.

We replaced 6 handoffs with 1 automated workflow.

The office manager told me she used to dread Monday mornings.

Now she gets through her queue before lunch.

If your team spends more time routing requests than resolving them, that's the bottleneck worth looking at.
```

**Structural Breakdown:**

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Setup | "6 weeks changed everything..." | Specific timeframe and scale |
| Before state | "Tenant request → email..." through "2.1 out of 5" | Arrows trace the broken flow, numbers quantify pain |
| After state | "Tenant request → AI triage..." through "4.4 out of 5" | Same arrow format shows the transformation |
| How | "Same team. Same budget. Same buildings." | Stacking pattern emphasizes nothing else changed |
| Human moment | "The office manager told me..." | Makes the data feel real |
| CTA | "If your team spends more time..." | Diagnostic framing, not a pitch |

### Template 4: Framework with Arrows

**Pillar:** EDU (Educational)

**Structure:** Problem framing → Arrow points → Summary → CTA

3-5 points, each preceded by a → arrow.

**Flow:**
```
Problem framing (2-3 lines)
↓
→ Point 1 (2-3 lines)
→ Point 2 (2-3 lines)
→ Point 3 (2-3 lines)
→ Point 4 (optional, 2-3 lines)
↓
Summary (1-2 lines)
↓
CTA (1-2 lines)
```

**Complete Example Post:**

```
Most small businesses fail at AI adoption for the same reason.

They automate the wrong thing first.

Here's the order that actually works:

→ Start with the task your team complains about most.

Not the one that sounds impressive to automate. The one that makes someone groan on Monday morning. That's your first win.

→ Measure the manual version before you touch anything.

How long does it take? How many steps? How often does it break? If you can't answer these, you're not ready to automate yet.

→ Build the smallest possible version.

One trigger. One action. One output. If it works, expand it. If it doesn't, you only wasted a day instead of a quarter.

→ Let the team adjust for 2 weeks before optimizing.

Every automation changes behavior. People need time to trust it. Optimizing too early means optimizing around fear, not around reality.

This order works because it builds trust before it builds complexity.

The businesses I've seen succeed with AI didn't start with the biggest problem.

They started with the most annoying one.

What's the task your team dreads most? That's probably where to start.
```

**Structural Breakdown:**

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Problem framing | "Most small businesses..." through "...the wrong thing first" | Names the mistake everyone makes |
| → Point 1 | "Start with the task..." | Counterintuitive first step |
| → Point 2 | "Measure the manual version..." | Readiness check most people skip |
| → Point 3 | "Build the smallest possible version..." | Scope control |
| → Point 4 | "Let the team adjust..." | Human factor most tech posts ignore |
| Summary | "This order works because..." | Ties the framework together |
| CTA | "What's the task your team dreads most?" | Activates reflection |

### Template 5: Hot Take / Contrarian

**Pillar:** TL (Thought Leadership)

**Structure:** Provocative claim → "Here's why" → Evidence 1, 2, 3 → The real point → CTA

Bold first line. Stacking evidence. Unexpected conclusion.

**Flow:**
```
Provocative claim (1 line)
↓
Pause line (1 line, optional)
↓
"Here's why" transition (1 line)
↓
Evidence 1 (2-3 lines)
Evidence 2 (2-3 lines)
Evidence 3 (2-3 lines)
↓
The real point (2-3 lines)
↓
CTA (1-2 lines)
```

**Complete Example Post:**

```
Stop calling it "AI transformation."

It's not transformation.

It's plumbing.

Here's why.

The businesses getting real results from AI aren't having visionary moments.

They're fixing leaky pipes.

A dentist's office was losing 40% of recall appointments because reminders went out by mail — 10 days before the appointment. By the time the postcard arrived, patients had already forgotten or booked elsewhere.

We replaced the postcards with automated texts sent 72 hours, 24 hours, and 2 hours before.

Recall rate went from 58% to 83%.

That's not transformation.

That's noticing where the water leaks and patching the hole.

A landscaping company spent 6 hours a week on invoice follow-ups. We automated the reminders. They got those hours back.

Not revolutionary.

Just functional.

The companies winning with AI right now aren't building the future.

They're fixing the present.

And that's worth way more.

What's the leakiest pipe in your business right now?
```

**Structural Breakdown:**

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Provocative claim | "Stop calling it AI transformation." | Pattern interrupt — challenges a popular phrase |
| "Here's why" | "Here's why." | Clean transition, earns continued reading |
| Evidence 1 | "A dentist's office..." through "...58% to 83%" | Specific story with numbers |
| Reframe | "That's not transformation. That's noticing..." | Reinforces the metaphor |
| Evidence 2 | "A landscaping company..." through "Just functional." | Second proof point, shorter for pacing |
| The real point | "The companies winning..." through "...worth way more" | Unexpected conclusion — mundane beats visionary |
| CTA | "What's the leakiest pipe..." | Uses the post's own metaphor |

### Template 6: Behind the Scenes

**Pillar:** SP/TL (Social Proof + Thought Leadership)

**Structure:** Setup → Raw details → The part nobody sees → What it means → CTA

Vulnerability without performing vulnerability.

**Flow:**
```
Setup (1-2 lines, time/place anchor)
↓
Raw details (4-6 lines of honest specifics)
↓
The part nobody sees (3-4 lines)
↓
What it means (2-3 lines)
↓
CTA (1-2 lines)
```

**Complete Example Post:**

```
Here's what 11 PM on a Wednesday looks like when you're building AI systems for small businesses.

Three browser tabs open.

One is a client's CRM that keeps timing out.

One is documentation for an API that was updated 4 days ago and broke the integration I built last month.

One is a Slack message from a client asking why their automated follow-ups stopped sending.

The answer took me 2 hours to find.

A single field name changed in the API update.

"contact_email" became "email_address."

That's it.

One renamed field shut down an entire client workflow for 16 hours before anyone noticed.

(Nobody posts about this part.)

The LinkedIn version of AI consulting is dashboards and demos.

The real version is hunting for a renamed field at 11 PM because someone's business depends on it.

I'm not complaining.

This is the work I chose when I left nursing.

But I want to be honest about what "building AI for small businesses" actually looks like.

It's not always the transformation story.

Sometimes it's the maintenance story.

And the maintenance story is where trust gets built.

If you're building something for clients and it feels unglamorous right now — keep going. That's the real work.
```

**Structural Breakdown:**

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Setup | "Here's what 11 PM on a Wednesday looks like..." | Time anchor creates immediacy |
| Raw details | "Three browser tabs..." through "...stopped sending" | Specific, unglamorous, real |
| The part nobody sees | "The answer took me 2 hours..." through "That's it." | Anticlimactic reveal is the point |
| Parenthetical aside | "(Nobody posts about this part.)" | Breaks the fourth wall, dry humor |
| What it means | "The LinkedIn version..." through "...trust gets built" | Reframes maintenance as trust |
| Personal thread | "I'm not complaining..." through "...left nursing" | Authentic identity anchor |
| CTA | "If you're building something..." | Solidarity, not selling |

## 4. Visual Formatting Checklist

Run every post through this checklist before output. All boxes must be checked.

- [ ] Every sentence on its own line?
- [ ] Blank line between every paragraph/thought?
- [ ] At least 2 short-punch lines (1-5 words)?
- [ ] At least 1 visual element (→, —, ...)?
- [ ] Hook earns the "see more" click?
- [ ] No markdown formatting (no **, no ##, no -)?
- [ ] Would this look good on a phone screen?
- [ ] No two visual elements combined in one sentence?
- [ ] Arrow count 4 or fewer?
- [ ] Em dash count 3 or fewer?
- [ ] Ellipsis count 2 or fewer?
- [ ] Lines stay under ~40 characters where possible?
